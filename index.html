Component Library

Account

This first component is from Amazon.com. I chose Amazon because I am always on Amazon! In 1995, Amazon.com began as an online bookstore before diversifying its product lines by adding VHSs, DVDs, music CDs, MP3s, computer software, video games, electronics, apparel, furniture, food, toys, and more. They offer a Prime Membership which allows members to enjoy faster shipping times and special sales.

Target Audience

People who shop on Amazon.com:

  • must have a credit/debit card
  • like to comparison shop
  • must have access to a computer with an internet connection
  • do not like to go to the mall
  • are digital natives, someone that is comfortable shopping online
  • are avid readers and Kindle owners
  • males or females

Client Goals & User Needs

Amazon's goals are simple. They want to make money and have users sign up for Prime memberships that will expand their customer base. User needs are to be able to find a product quickly, compare prices, and have it shipped to their house fast.

Amazon also wants to make sure that the user has as much input and freedom over their account information without having to call for support. Amazon does a great job of this by having several categories for users to edit. Some websites have the user call customer service if they need to change their name, address, or password. With Amazon's extensive member management tools, the user can do most everything on their own.

Critique

The account information is placed in the header where it is easy to find. The user can check on account info, membership info, wish lists, and their shopping cart. Amazon used a scroll over tab feature to show these different sections. When you scroll over the tabs, a list of information drops down. Scrolling over "Your Account" (shown in the image above), drops down in a list for you to choose what you want to do. The text is a very dark gray, which is professional and mature, while sub-categories are lighter gray (practical and quality) and smaller font. This shows hierarchy. To the left is a "buy it again" section. This shows the last few items purchased on this account to make it easier for re-purchases or to keep track of when and what has been purchased. The text for this is a bright blue to differentiate from the account information. The price is written in red to make it pop. The "Buy it Again" text, as well as part of the "prime" icons, are orange which promote action.

Blog

This is a blog section from filehippo.com. They offer the latest free downloadable software, updates and drivers.

Target Audience

People who use this website are:

  • computer users
  • searching for a specific piece of software
  • need an update
  • searching for the latest and best
  • want free and safe software
  • looking to share their opinions or knowledge
  • want the latest tech news

Client Goals & User Needs

FileHippo's goals are to offer the latest, safest, free downloadable software, drivers and updates as well as technical news and opinions. The user's goals are to find that information.

Critique

This blog/news section is just that, a section. It's located to the right of the CTA. As your eye follows the page in a left to right fashion, this is the second place your eye stops. The title is a bold dark gray font (which shows maturity and professionalism) while the different article titles are not bold in a lighter shade of gray (which is practical and quality).This shows hierarchy. The use of a newspaper icon next to the title, (in trustworthy blue), shows the user that this is the section for the latest news. The blog section also has pictures of what the article is about to draw you in even further. By teasing you with a picture of an angry Droid, you want to find out what is making him angry.

Contact

This contact page is from Walgreens. I chose it because of it's detail.

Target Audience

The target audience for Walgreens would include:

  • customers who want to refill or set up automatic refills for a perscription
  • members of the "Balance Rewards" program
  • travelers that want to find the closest store
  • people who want to develop film or upload pictures
  • sick people looking for the in-house clinic
  • people needing health information or advice

Client Goals & User Needs

Walgreens wants to increase their customer numbers and provide an easy way to order/re-order medication when the customer is sick. They also want to provide important information about the locations of their stores and walk-in clinics. Users would like to shop from home when they are not well or injured and they want to be able to find the closest store in case of an emergency.

Critique

The contact information is located in the footer under customer service, which is a very common place to find this. Once you open the contact us option, (see image above) there are categories to guide you to the right place. Walgreens has eleven different categories of contact. Within these eleven categories are between 3 and 8 sub-categories. When the user clicks on a sub-category, another 3 to 6 categories drop down. This is one the most specific contact pages I have ever seen.

All of these categories and sub-categories are aligned in three columns. This makes it easier to follow and look through each list. I would like this contact page more if the categories were alphabetized. Each title is a black font that, in my opinion, should be bold, but is not. The subs are a bright blue which promote trust.
